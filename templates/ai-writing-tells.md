# Flat-Prose Patterns (Template)

> **Purpose:** A style guide to the language, rhythm, and formatting habits that make prose read as generic, inflated, or default — the reflexes to strip out so the writing reads as *this book* and not as anyone's first draft. Lives at `style/ai-writing-tells.md`.
>
> **Why it matters here:** Every chapter is drafted fast, and fast drafting reaches for the nearest phrasing — which is almost always the flattest. These are the patterns that phrasing falls into. The **Writer** avoids them while drafting; the **Editor** hunts for them on review.
>
> **Relationship to the tic blocklist:** `style/tic-blocklist.md` tracks tics *specific to this book* caught in the act. This file is the *general* catalogue of flat-prose habits that apply before a single chapter exists. A pattern that keeps recurring in this book should be promoted to a concrete entry in the tic blocklist.
>
> **How to read this:** These are *observations, not laws*. Any single item can appear in strong prose. The problem is the **clustering** — several of these, repeatedly, is what drains a page. Fix the underlying flatness, not just the surface marker.

## 1. Inflation & false significance

- **Undue emphasis on significance / legacy / broader trends.** Puffing up how a moment "marked a turning point," "shaped the era," "left an indelible mark," or fits a "broader movement." In fiction this surfaces as the narrator inflating the stakes of an ordinary beat. *Words to watch:* stands/serves as, is a testament/reminder, pivotal moment, marking a shift, evolving landscape, setting the stage for, deeply rooted, enduring legacy.
- **Superficial analysis via trailing participles.** A sentence states a fact, then bolts on an `-ing` clause that editorializes its meaning: "…, highlighting its importance," "…, reflecting a deeper tension," "…, cementing her role." Cut the trailing gloss; let the fact stand.
- **Vague attributions / overgeneralized opinion.** "Observers noted," "many believed," "it was widely seen as," "critics argued" — authority with no owner. In a scene, attribute perception to the POV character or cut it.
- **Promotional / travel-brochure register.** "Nestled in the heart of," "boasts a rich and vibrant," "a breathtaking tapestry of." The prose sells the setting instead of inhabiting it.
- **Outline-shaped "challenges and future" summaries.** "Despite these hardships, X endured, a reminder that…" — a tidy upbeat wrap-up that restates the theme. Novels rarely need the paragraph that tells you what the last paragraph meant.

## 2. Word choice & sentence shape

- **High density of default vocabulary.** A recognizable cluster of overused, reach-for-first words. One is nothing; a fistful drains the page: *delve, tapestry, testament, underscore, showcase, boasts, vibrant, intricate, meticulous, pivotal, crucial, landscape (abstract), realm, foster, enhance, garner, interplay, nuanced, robust, seamless, navigate (abstract), resonate, embark, beacon, stark reminder, rich (history/tradition).*
- **Avoidance of plain copulatives.** Replacing *is / are / has* with inflated substitutes: *serves as, stands as, represents, marks, boasts, features, offers, embodies.* "The room was cold" is stronger than "The room served as a stark reminder of the cold."
- **Negative parallelisms.** The signature contrast-for-effect constructions: "Not just X, but Y," "It wasn't X — it was Y," "not a mirror but a portal," "X rather than Y." Occasionally earned; as a reflex, hollow.
- **Rule of three.** Compulsive triplets — "adjective, adjective, and adjective," or three parallel clauses — used to *sound* comprehensive. Break the pattern; two is often truer, and one can be sharper.
- **Elegant variation.** Refusing to repeat a word, so a character becomes "the young woman," then "the brunette," then "the newcomer" in three sentences. A reflex, not a style. Repeat the plain word when it's the right word.
- **Sudden shifts in register** that belong to no character — clinical and gushing in the same breath, or the diction lurching between chapters.

## 3. Formatting habits

- **Overuse of em dashes** — especially spaced, and especially to stage a "punchy" reveal at a clause's end. Prefer commas, periods, or a colon unless the dash genuinely earns the interruption.
- **Overuse of boldface / inline-header lists.** "**Key point:** …" bullet formatting bleeding into prose. Fiction is prose; a list inside a scene almost always breaks it.
- **Title Case In Headings** where sentence case is the house style, and thematic breaks (`---`) sprinkled before every heading.
- **Curly vs. straight quotation marks and apostrophes used inconsistently** within the same passage. Pick the convention from the writing guidelines and hold it.
- **Emoji as decoration**, section labels, or bullet ornaments — never inside the prose of this book.

## 4. Register leakage (must never reach the page)

Draft artifacts that address a reader or a collaborator instead of staying inside the story. Any of these in a chapter file is an automatic blocker.

- Collaborative filler: "Certainly!", "Of course!", "I hope this helps," "Would you like me to…", "Let me know if…", "Here is a…".
- Sourcing / knowledge disclaimers: "As of my last update," "while specific details are limited," "based on available information," "not widely documented." (In-world, a character can lack knowledge; the *narrator* citing missing sources cannot.)
- Placeholders left unfilled: "[Describe the scene here]," "[Character's Name]," "insert detail," "TODO."
- Didactic hedges aimed at a reader: "It's important to note that…," "it's worth remembering that…," "keep in mind that…."
- Meta section summaries: "In summary," "In conclusion," "Overall," closing a chapter by restating it.

## 5. Not real problems (do not "fix" these into blandness)

Chasing these degrades good prose or triggers false alarms:

- **Perfect grammar** on its own. It is not a flaw.
- **Formal or "fancy" vocabulary** in general — the problem is the *specific* overused words above, not difficulty or elegance.
- **A single em dash, a single triplet, a single "not X but Y."** Isolated use is normal craft. Clustering is the signal.
- **Mixed clinical/emotional register**, which for an unusual POV (AI, child, alien) may be exactly the intended voice — see `style/voice-signature.md`.

## Workflow

1. **Writer, while drafting:** keep §§1–4 in mind; §4 items must never appear at all. When you catch yourself reaching for a §2 word or a §3 pattern, choose the plainer, more specific alternative.
2. **Editor, on review:** sweep explicitly for clustering. Report *patterns*, not single instances (except §4, which is always a blocker). Where a pattern recurs across chapters, open or update a concrete entry in `style/tic-blocklist.md`.
3. **Protected exceptions:** if a flagged construction is diegetically required (e.g. a character who genuinely talks in triads), record it in `open-points/diegesis-protection-register.md` so a later pass does not "correct" it.
